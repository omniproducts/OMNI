The Day Of The Jackal

The Jackal(Alexander Duggan)

Background & Military History- Alexander James Gordon Duggan (often "Alex Duggan") is a former British Army sniper who trained at Sandhurst before enlisting in the Parachute Regiment. He advanced to the elite Pathfinder Platoon, seeing action in Northern Ireland, Norway, Kosovo, Sierra Leone, and Iraq, and later joined a UKSF unit in Afghanistan. During a mission in 2013 in Helmand Province, his team committed a war crime—massacring civilians disguised as a wedding party. Outraged, Duggan detonated an IED that killed his squad (except his spotter), faked his own death, and vanished.

Transition to Assassin- Post-military, Duggan became a highly skilled freelance assassin. He adopted the codename “The Jackal”, borrowing it from an armored vehicle from his former unit (paired with “Phantom”) rather than it being a personal nickname. He uses a rotating roster of aliases—over a dozen—including Charles Calthrop (the name known to his wife), Ralf, Anthony Mallinson, and others, to seamlessly vanish between identities.

Personal Life & Dual Identity- Under the alias Charles Calthrop, Duggan lives a double life: married to Nuria (Spanish wife), with whom he has a young son named Carlito. Nuria believes him to be a corporate troubleshooter. Despite his calculated ruthlessness, this family bond introduces unexpected complexity to his character.

Skills & Persona- The Jackal is a meticulous planner, expert marksman, master of disguise, and coldly logical operator. In the series, he pulls off an almost unbelievable sniper kill at 3,815 meters—an act so unbelievable even MI6 initially doubts it. His ruthlessness is chilling: collateral casualties don’t faze him, emphasizing his focus on the mission. Yet there’s an added vulnerability and nuance: the show explores his conflict between lethal efficiency and his hidden family life.

Bianca Pullman

Who is Bianca Pullman? Bianca Pullman, portrayed by Lashana Lynch, is a former police officer turned firearms expert in Section 303 of MI6. She leads the investigation into the Jackal's high-profile assassinations. A skilled analyst, she quickly spotlights the Jackal’s customized suitcase-sniper rifle and strategically links it to a Northern Irish gunsmith—driving the central cat-and-mouse chase of the series.

Personality & Motivations- Biographically, she’s portrayed as relentless, resourceful, and often uncompromising. She’s described by her colleagues as a “pain in the arse,” yet her instincts and persistence make her indispensable to the case. In an interview, the show’s writers characterize her as an outsider—like Lebel from the original story—and someone who shirks niceties and platitudes. Her commitment to the job often clashes with her personal life, introducing a powerful duality. The Guardian

Criticized as Unlikable- Many viewers found her abrasive, emotionally distant, and self-serving. Some described her as “passive-aggressive,” “reckless,” or burnout personified. “She is written as the mirror image of the Jackal—ruthless, ambitious, egoistic, job above everything-else.”

Intentional Moral Ambiguity- Others argue the character is crafted deliberately to mirror the Jackal’s complexity—flawed, driven, but compelling. “Bianca is undeniably flawed... she’s a woman driven to succeed... relentless, rude and dogged.” “I enjoy her character... the lines between good and bad are blurred.”

Nuria

Nuria (played by Úrsula Corberó) is the Jackal’s Spanish wife, who knows him under the alias Charles Calthrop. She is depicted as a devoted partner and mother, sharing a child, Carlito, with him. Nuria’s character is caught between her love and loyalty to her husband and the moral and emotional complexities that arise as she learns more about his secret life as a high-profile assassin. She provides a glimpse into the Jackal’s personal, more human side, contrasting with his cold, methodical professional persona.

Nuria primarily represents the Jackal’s personal life, grounding him outside his deadly profession. She interacts with him under his alias, unaware at first of his true identity. Her relationship with him, and her role as a mother to Carlito, humanizes the otherwise cold and meticulous assassin, showing that he has a private, vulnerable side. Throughout the series, her presence creates tension—she is a reminder of the normal life the Jackal could have but also a potential risk if she ever discovers his secret. Essentially, Nuria is a bridge between the Jackal’s hidden life and the emotional consequences of his actions, making her a subtle but important character in understanding him beyond his professional persona.

Ulle Dag Charles(UDC)

Ulle Dag Charles is depicted as a tech billionaire and activist who becomes one of the Jackal’s targets in the unfolding plot. His character adds complexity to the narrative, intertwining themes of technology, power, and the global implications of the Jackal's assignments.

Ulle Dag Charles is depicted as a charismatic and visionary entrepreneur. He is the founder of a revolutionary software called River, designed to bring unprecedented transparency to global financial systems. River aims to expose hidden wealth and illicit financial activities, challenging the status quo of the world's elite. This bold initiative makes him a significant threat to powerful financial interests. Despite his status as the world's richest man, Charles is portrayed as someone who is not concerned about potential assassination attempts. His confidence and public persona suggest a man who believes in his mission and is prepared to face any challenges that come his way.

The character's name, Ulle Dag Charles, is an anagram of Charles de Gaulle, the historical French president who was the original target in Frederick Forsyth's 1971 novel. This naming choice serves as a modern parallel, positioning Charles as a contemporary figure challenging established power structures, much like de Gaulle did in his time.

As the Jackal is hired to eliminate Charles, the series delves into themes of power, transparency, and the lengths to which individuals and organizations will go to protect their interests. Charles's character serves as a catalyst for the unfolding events, driving the narrative forward and highlighting the intersection of technology, wealth, and political intrigue.
